1. Statistical Test¶

1.1 Statistical test used to analyze the NYC subway data.¶

To figure out if more people ride the subway when it is raining versus when it is not raining, I separated the data into two groups: rain and norain, and performed the Mann–Whitney U test:

Denoting the ENTRIESn_hourly variable, as E:

$$E = ENTRIESn\_hourly$$

The null hypothesis is that there is no difference in ridership. The observed differences between means is due to chance:

$$H_0: \overline{E_{rain}} = \overline{E_{norain}}$$

The alternative hypothesis is that there is a real difference between both means. Either group can have a larger mean and the difference between them can't be explained by chance only.

$$H_A: \overline{E_{rain}} \neq \overline{E_{norain}}$$

I need to determine with 95% confidence if less or more people ride the subway when it is raining versus when it is not raining. For this reason:

$$p-critical = 0.05,\ two\ tails$$

1.2 Applicability of the test to the dataset.¶

The Mann Whitney U test is applicable to the dataset because it adheres to the test assumptions, in particular the following:

  • The two groups under consideration are random and independent of each other, as well as the observations within each group.

  • The observations are numeric and can be arranged in ranks (i.e. one can say of two observations which is greater).

  • This dataset has a non-normal, highly skewed distribution (Fig 3.1). Most of the observations are in the lower end (0-6000), and it has a big tail with a maximum value of 32814 entries. For this reason the applicability of the t-test is questionable.

  • When separating the dataset in two groups, rain and norain, the distribution of both groups is very similar (Fig. 3.2). Judging just by the relative frequencies of both groups, it is not possible to tell any difference in ridership.

1.4 Significance and interpretation of the results¶

With these results, I can reject the null hypothesis with a 95% confidence in favor of the alternative hypothesis: the differences observed between the two groups cannot be explained just by chance. There is a real difference between both distributions.

  • I chose station over UNIT because it is easier to think of station names than turnstile UNIT numbers, even though the $r^2$ would drop slightly. These variables are related (the UNITs are located in the stations). So, there is no reason to select both at the same time. These last two variables are also related, or dependent on each other.
  • I chose the variable rain over conds even though I think that the weather conditions are better expressed with the latter, because after computing the correlation and testing the model to make some predictions, the predictions tended to produce the opposite result than expected. e.g. There were more entries on Clear conditions than under Light or Heavy Rain.
  • I also included the variables tempi and wdspdi because temperature, especially low temperature, and the effect that wind speed can have in our sensation of it, could well provoke someone's decision to ride the subway instead of a more exposed way of travelling.

2.6 Goodness of fit for the regression model¶

To try to determine how well the model fits the data, or how well it will predict a future set of observations so as to be considered useful, I will examine the coefficient of determination and the residuals.

$R^2$¶

The coefficient of determination, $R^2 = 0.43029$ is an indicator of the stregth of the relationship between the variables. It means that $43.0\%$ of the variations observed in ENTRIESn_hourly can be explained due to the variation in the regressor variables chosen: hour, weekday, rain, tempi, wspdi, station. The other $57.0\%$ is due to the influence of other variables, by randomness, or by the model chosen (in this case a linear model). $R^2$ can be considered as related to the unexplained variance of the model.

Residuals¶

The residuals —the difference between the observed data and the predicted values— are a measure of the model's errors. To examine these residuals I drew a histogram (Fig 2.1), a plot (Fig 2.2) of residuals and a quantiles-quantiles plot (Fig 2.3)

From these graphs I observe the following:

  • The histogram of the residuals has long asymmetrical tails. The tail in the positive side is much longer than the negative tail.
  • The distribution of the residuals is not "normal". I is positively skewed and has extreme values.
  • The plot hints some periodic or cyclic pattern, which is not very clear or easy to identify.
  • The Q-Q plot shows three regions: a central region with closer fit, and two outer regions where the distribution of residuals departs significantly from a normal distribution.

Conclusion¶

Considering the results obtained in the linear regression analysis —the value of $R^2$ and the graphs of the residuals— I conclude that the linear model is not appropriate for the regression analysis of this dataset. The model fits the data only in one portion of the data, but not for the whole dataset. The model predicts values that tend to be larger than the observed ones.

2.7 Example of Predictions using the Model¶

The formula for the model, to make predictions, is:

$\hat{y} = a + B \cdot x$

$\begin{eqnarray*}\ Where: &\ & a & = & intercept\ & B & = & coefficients\ & x & = & values\ for\ each\ of\ the\ regressor\ variables\ & \hat{y} & = & predicted\ ENTRIESn_hourly\ \end{eqnarray*}$

In [13]:
from collections import OrderedDict as OD

def predict(hour, weekday, rain, tempi, wspdi, station):
    x_dummies = OD(zip(dummies.columns, [0]*len(dummies)))
    if 'station_'+station not in x_dummies:
        print('station "{}" not recognized in the model'.format(station))
        return
    x_dummies['station_'+station] = 1
    x = [hour, weekday, rain, tempi, wspdi]
    x.extend(x_dummies.values())
    y_hat = a + np.dot(B,x)
    return y_hat

4. Conclusions¶

  1. From the analysis and interpretation of this dataset I can conclude that there is a difference in ridership between both groups studied: rain and norain. The difference is significant and can't be explained only by chance.
  2. The Mann-Whitney U test performed on both groups indicate that the probability (p < .001) is so small as to explain the observed differences in ridership by randomness alone.
  3. We can conclude with a 95% confidence that one of the groups has a larger mean. When it is rainy, the mean is 2028.2, compared to 1845.5 when it is not raining. There is a difference of 182.7 more entries when it is rainy.
  4. The regression analyisis computed shows consistently an increase in ridership when there are rainy conditions.
  5. Based on these results we can conclude that more people ride the NYC subway when it rains as compared when it does not rain.

5. Reflection¶

5.1 Potential shortcomings¶

Limitations of the Dataset¶

The dataset analyzed contains data only for the month of May 2011. It is during spring, and typically rainy with mild temperatures in NYC. For the analysis to be representative and useful year round, we should have data from at least one whole year.

The dataset contains data for 207 out of the 421 NYC subway stations. Some of the subway stations are located too far from their respective weather station (13 stations are more than 4km away).

The dataset contains very extreme values. They may due to special events like baseball games or concerts, where at the finishig time there could be an extra 10,000 or 20,000 people on the subway, snow, rain or shine. I think these large values should be considered both in the statistical analysis and in the regression analysis.

About the hour variable, the hours chosen when the transformation of the original data was made are: [0, 4, 8, 12, 16, 20], each corresponding to the ending hour of a 4 hour period. My concern is about the first number: 0 which has the observations from 20:00 the day before, to 0:00 today. I think the correct values should be [4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24], all of the same day. In Fig. 3.5 I had to put hour 0 at the end of the graph for it to make sense.

Limitations of the Analysis¶

The presence of extreme values in this dataset is very important and carry valuable information that should be taken into consideration. They are not "bad points" but data with information that should be investigated carefully.

In this analysis, however they produce a bias in the result. Not so much in the Mann-Whitney test because the method assigns a rank to each value, regardless of the high difference in value between them.

In the linear regression, on the other hand, the extreme values pose a heavy weight on each coefficient, producing a predicted value larger than it really is. The linear model is not
